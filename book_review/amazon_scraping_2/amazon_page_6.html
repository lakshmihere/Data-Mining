kkth1866
3.0 out of 5 starsA compelling psychological portrait of one woman's Stockholm syndrome
Reviewed in the United States on November 3, 2017
Despite being the title figure in this book, Madame Chic is a shadowy figure who only pops into life at odd intervals to humiliate her young charge. When the naive Jennifer Scott makes the mistake of thinking it doesn't matter what she wears to bed because no one will see it, Madame Chic is there to wrinkle her nose in mock-confusion to why she would own well-worn sweatpants for pajamas. When Scott - so young, so americaine - foolishly believes that it's sufficient when making a homemade fruit tart for the menfolk as dessert on a Wednesday night to just, you know, put the fruit on the tart, Madame Chic is there to reprimand her in horror because it must be arranged *artistically.* When the twenty-something Scott hopefully trots next to Madame Chic on her errands, wondering if the formidable woman is starting to warm up to her, Madame Chic breaks the ice but telling her she looks terrible in the color she is wearing. And on.
And on.

The strangest thing is that Scott likes this! The French have all the virtues we vulgar Americans lack. They never watch TV. (Don't tell that to my house mother ten years ago who was an avid fan of NCIS). They love culture. They only wear cashmere and silk. They know how to savor food. They live passionately.

It's not surprising Scott fell victim to these cliches. Lots of young Americans abroad for the first time go through a period where they rebel against the perceived vulgarity of America in favor of some ideal if Europe. It's not even necessarily bad that Scott took from France lessons about resisting consumerism and buying quality.

What is surprising is that ten years after the fact Scott has failed to come to terms with the fact that Madame Chic was an unkind, judgmental person and that being an unkind, judgmental person is central to the whole French chic fad as peddled in the US.

French chic is all about there being one way to live and dress. There's no room for a punk aesthetic or a love of loud, vibrant jewelry; it's boatneck or bust. When a husband is offended that the strawberries on his tart aren't arranged in a Fibonacci spiral, he's exercising a praiseworthy attention to detail, not being a jerk to his wife. The only way you get a culture that internalizes these norms and attention to quality is by empowering bitter, judgmental women like Madame Chic to slap down young women who step outside the rules. Because keeping your mouth shut, even when you dislike their pajamas, to make them feel welcome? So not chic.

Cynthia Meyer
4.0 out of 5 stars Rigolo et amusant
Reviewed in the United States on February 20, 2012
Verified Purchase
Lessons from Madame Chic is a breezy, clever and entertaining read. I discovered it entirely by accident, and purchased the book after reading the free sample. It is more than worth the modest price. Madame Chic is a quick read; I finished it in about 90 minutes.

Ms. Scott writes with the language and style of a magazine journalist, with tongue-in-cheek social commentary mixed with Californian self-improvement.

I agree with some of the comments that the author could benefit from an editor. As a French and English speaker, I found some of the franglais to be way too campy (e.g. le recap) Ms. Scott has a lot of raw talent and merits a relationship with a good publishing house. She has bestseller potential.

One note: the target audience for this book are clearly twenty-somethings. That did not stop mefrom enjoying the book but it was clearly directed at a younger reader.

Well I am a recent fan of all things French; my exposure to anything French being limited to a few long layovers in France. However I still distinctly remember being struck by a sense of everything being being put together "just so" in all the women (of course the stewardesses). I read French Women Don't Get Fat and I was charmed and wanted to explore the genre further, so I read Jennifer's book. Her delivery is supple, succinct and pretty with short sweet sentences. I loved the philosophies, got many tips and have sort of rediscovered the simple joys around me. It is hard to explain what the book is, if you are not familiar with this specific genre. This book has improved my daily quality of life in many ways. I think you should read it. It is different and honestly written and I believe you will like it.

3.0 out of 5 stars ... spend that "semester abroad" in the home of someone like Madame Chic
Reviewed in the United States on November 29, 2014
Verified Purchase
It would be lovely if our children were able to spend that "semester abroad" in the home of someone like Madame Chic. I think that the value of this book was for the author, as a young woman having the opportunity of being in the right place at the right time. While Madame Chic does not in my opinion represent all French women, her essence does and without a doubt, many young women would benefit for being hosted in a home such as hers. To her credit, she learned a lot in that home and that to me was the best part of the book. She probably learned more from living in Madame Chic's house than from the studies she went to Paris to pursue! I'm glad that she took heed of that example. Mrs. Chic seems to have it all together. For me as a reader, her advice on the art of living is not surprising or out of the ordinary. Elegance and grace comes from within all of us if we let it. The "little things" that we do daily can certainly contribute to make us happy, satisfied and confident. We in turn pass that on to the people we live with and the children we raise.
